II. The War of the Second Coalition

Britain and Austria organized a new coalition against France in 1798, which included Russia for the first time, although nothing significant happened until 1799, except for the expedition to Naples. Another expedition was sent to County Mayo to support the revolt against Britain in the summer of 1798. It was successful against the British forces, most notably at Castlebar. French ships sent to assist them were captured by the Royal Navy near County Donegal.

1799
In Europe, the allies organized several invasions, including campaigns in Italy and Switzerland and an Anglo-Russian invasion of the Netherlands. Russian General Alexander Suvorov imposed a series of defeats on France in Italy, pushing them back into the Alps. However, the allies were less victorious in the Netherlands, where Britain withdrew after a stalemate (although they managed to capture the Dutch fleet), and in Switzerland, where, after some victories, the Russian army was completely defeated in the Second Battle of Zurich.

1800
Napoleon sent Moreau on a campaign in Germany, while he himself went to recruit a new army in Dijon and marched through Switzerland to attack the Austrian armies in Italy from behind. Barely avoiding defeat, he defeated Austria at Marengo and reoccupied northern Italy. Meanwhile, Moreau invaded Bavaria and won a major battle against Austria at Hohenlinden. Moreau continued to advance towards Vienna, forcing Austria to seek peace.

In Egypt, the Ottomans and the British advanced and finally forced the French forces to surrender after the fall of Cairo and Alexandria. Britain continued the war at sea. A coalition of neutral states including Prussia, Russia, Denmark, and Sweden intervened to protect neutral naval transports against the British blockade. The British fleet achieved a victory through Nelson's surprise attack against the Danish fleet in the port of Copenhagen.

1802
Britain signed the Treaty of Amiens, ending the war and recognizing French conquests. Thus began the longest period of peace between 1792 and 1815. This moment marks the transition from the French Revolutionary Wars to the Napoleonic Wars, with Napoleon being crowned emperor in 1804.
