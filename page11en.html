VIII. Hundred Days

The Hundred Days period refers to the interval between Napoleon Bonaparte's return to Paris on March 20, 1815, after his exile to Elba, and the restoration of King Louis XVIII on July 8, 1815. During this period, the Sixth Coalition was formed, which included Austria, Prussia, Russia, the United Kingdom, and several German states, with the aim of defeating Napoleon. The coalition mobilized nearly a million soldiers, with 85,000 Austrian and Piedmontese troops in northern and central Italy, 168,000 Russian soldiers heading towards central Germany, 225,000 Austrian and German troops stationed along the Rhine, and 94,600 Anglo-allied troops in Belgium under the command of the Duke of Wellington.

Napoleon's plan was to divide the enemy forces and defeat them separately. He aimed to first defeat the Anglo-allied and Prussian armies in Belgium before turning against the Austrian and Russian forces. Napoleon's maneuver through Charleroi, involving striking between the two enemy armies and cutting their communication lines, was a success. This strategic move was possible because the Coalition had not anticipated that the French would use this difficult route, and the Prussian and Anglo-allied armies were caught off guard by the French attack. Despite Napoleon's victories at Ligny and Quatre Bras, he failed to achieve a decisive victory, and the Prussian and Anglo-allied armies managed to retreat. Napoleon then made a fatal mistake by dividing his army and sending a large part with Marshal Grouchy to pursue the retreating Prussians, leaving himself outnumbered at Waterloo. The Battle of Waterloo, fought on June 18, 1815, ended in a decisive victory for the Coalition, and Napoleon was forced to abdicate for the second time.
