V.1 War of the Third Coalition 1805

Napoleon Bonaparte conceived a massive plan to invade the British Isles, gathering 180,000 soldiers at Boulogne. In order to achieve this, he attempted to obtain naval superiority, but his plans proved to be unsuccessful. The Franco-Spanish fleet led by Admiral Villeneuve was defeated in the Battle of Trafalgar by Horatio Nelson, the latter losing his life in the battle. Napoleon then abandoned his plan to invade Britain and redirected his attention to enemies on the continent. The naval and colonial conflict that preceded these events influenced Napoleon's decision to abandon the invasion of Britain. The conflict in the Caribbean and the occupation of colonial bases directly impacted European clashes. This context led to an escalation of the Napoleonic conflict into what historians call a world war.

As Austria joined the coalition against France, Napoleon faced an Austrian army of approximately 70,000 men. During the Ulm Campaign, he managed to encircle and force the surrender of the Austrian army led by Karl Mack von Leiberich. Afterward, he occupied Vienna and faced a numerically superior Russo-Austrian army. December 1805 brought decisive victory for Napoleon in the Battle of Austerlitz, after which Austria signed the Treaty of Pressburg, ceding Venice and Tyrol. With Austria's withdrawal from the coalition, Napoleon became dominant on the continent, although the Russian army had not yet fully entered the conflict.
