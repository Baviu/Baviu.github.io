II.1 The War of the First Coalition

The War of the First Coalition was a conflict between the First French Republic and several European powers between 1792 and 1797. Although the European powers collectively were superior to France, they were not always coordinated in their actions due to various reasons, including divergent regional interests. The war began with France's declaration of war against the Habsburg monarchy in April 1792. In July of the same year, a Prussian army entered France but was defeated at the Battle of Valmy. The coalition powers subsequently invaded France several times, and France suffered some defeats but none decisive. France responded with drastic measures, such as the introduction of the "lev√©e en masse," which required all potential male recruits aged 18-25 to join the army. The new French armies managed to repel the invaders and advance beyond French territory.

In May 1795, the French established the Batavian Republic as a sister republic and gained Prussian recognition of French control over the left bank of the Rhine through the Peace of Basel. With the Treaty of Campo Formio, the Holy Roman Empire ceded Austrian Netherlands to France, and northern Italy was transformed into several client republics of the First French Republic. Spain separately concluded a peace agreement with France (known as the Second Treaty of Basel), and the French Directory made plans to conquer more of the Holy Roman Empire of that period. To the north of the Alps, Archduke Charles, Duke of Teschen, somewhat reversed the situation in 1796, but Napoleon blocked his advance in what was termed the "resolution of northern Italy," including defeating the temporary coalition of the Kingdom of Sardinia and Austria in northern Italy (1796-1797) near the Po Valley, culminating in the Treaties of Leoben and Campo Formio (in October 1797). Thus, the First Coalition against France collapsed, leaving only Great Britain in the fight against France.
