II.1 The War of the First Coalition

Since 1791, the monarchies of Europe have been closely monitoring the evolution of the French Revolution, wondering whether they should intervene to support Louis XVI or take advantage of the chaos in the country. The Holy Roman Emperor, Leopold II, brother of Marie Antoinette, was a key figure in this context. Initially, Leopold viewed the French Revolution calmly, but he became increasingly disturbed by the radicalization of the situation. Eventually, he and King Frederick William II of Prussia issued the Declaration of Pillnitz on August 27, expressing their concern for the well-being of Louis and his family and threatening serious consequences in case of action against them. Although initially conceived as a means to buy time and avoid open conflict with France, this declaration was interpreted as a serious threat by the French revolutionary leaders.

In addition to the ideological tensions between France and the European monarchies, there were also territorial disputes in Alsace and concerns about the departure of French nobles into exile. France declared war on Austria in April 1792, and the invasion of the southern Netherlands was planned by the Foreign Minister Dumouriez. However, the French army was poorly organized, and recruitment was insufficient. During the invasion, French soldiers deserted en masse, and the Prussian army quickly captured the fortresses of Longwy and Verdun. Despite these challenging beginnings, the French achieved a significant victory at Valmy in September 1792, repelling the Prussian invasion and bolstering confidence in the Revolution. The defeat of the Prussians led to their withdrawal from France and the abolition of the French monarchy. At the same time, French forces advanced into Italy and Germany, occupying important regions.
