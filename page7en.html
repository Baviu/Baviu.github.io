V.2 War of the Forth Coalition 1805

The Fourth Coalition, formed in 1806-1807, represented an attempt to oppose Napoleon's expansion and French power. It included Prussia, the Russian Empire, Saxony, Sweden, and the United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland. The coalition quickly formed after the collapse of the Third Coalition. In July 1806, Napoleon created the Confederation of the Rhine, turning several German states into duchies and kingdoms under French influence, thus facilitating the governance of non-Prussian Germany. In August, the King of Prussia decided to enter the war independently of the support of other major powers. Berlin was captured in record time, and Napoleon issued decrees laying the groundwork for the Continental System, a commercial blockade against the United Kingdom.

Through the Treaty of Tilsit in 1807, Tsar Alexander I was forced to make peace with Napoleon, and Russia became a temporary ally of France. Subsequently, Napoleon focused on driving Russian forces out of Poland and created the Duchy of Warsaw. In the battles of Eylau and Friedland, the Russian armies were defeated, and the tsar was forced to sign the Treaty of Tilsit. At the Congress of Erfurt, Napoleon and Alexander agreed to compel Sweden to adhere to the Continental System, triggering the Finnish War of 1808-1809 and dividing Sweden into two parts. Thus, the Coalition had a significant impact on European politics and events during that period, marking a crucial moment in the confrontation with Napoleon's expansion.
