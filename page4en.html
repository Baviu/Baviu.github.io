III. Napoleon's campaign in Egypt

Napoleon's campaign in Egypt, which took place from 1798 to 1801, was a French expedition to establish a colony in Egypt and to threaten British possessions in India. Despite initial victories, the campaign ultimately ended in failure. The expedition is notable for the discovery of the Rosetta Stone and the birth of modern Egyptology.

The French army, led by General Napoleon Bonaparte, was initially successful. After a brief stop at Malta, where they took control of the island, the French arrived in Alexandria and captured the city. They then marched to Cairo, defeating a Mamluk army along the way in the Battle of the Pyramids. However, the French suffered a significant defeat at the Battle of the Nile, which took place on August 1, 1798. In this battle, the French fleet was almost completely destroyed by the British Navy, led by Admiral Horatio Nelson. This defeat cut off the French army from any supplies or reinforcements from France. Despite these setbacks, Napoleon attempted to win over the Egyptian populace by engaging in theological discussions with local sheiks and presenting himself as a liberator. He also funded a three-day celebration of the Prophet Muhammad's birthday and established the Institut d'Egypte. Despite these efforts, the campaign ultimately ended in failure.
